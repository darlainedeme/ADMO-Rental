\section{The apartment}\label{sec:apt}

\eyebrow{The space, in plain words}

A 599 sq ft (about 56 m\textsuperscript{2}) furnished one-bedroom in a small four-unit brick building from 1900. Open-plan kitchen and living area, a generous double-vanity bathroom with a big walk-in shower, sliding barn doors to the bedroom, projector for movie nights on the big white wall, in-unit laundry, central A/C. The building stays quiet, the neighborhood doesn't, and you'll figure out pretty quickly that's the point.

\subsection*{What it's like}

It's unit 4 in a three-story brick condo from 1900. Just four apartments in the whole building, and neighbors who actually say hi in the stairwell. That bit matters more than people realize: this isn't a 200-unit tower where you ride the elevator next to strangers. There's nobody upstairs (you're on the top floor, so no one stomps on your head), and you'll know who lives in front and downstairs by the second week.

Inside, the kitchen and living area share one open, light-flooded room with two big windows. The windows do not face 18th Street, which is the point: you get the daylight without the sidewalk noise. \textbf{The apartment gets a remarkable amount of natural light}, especially in the afternoon, the kind that makes plants thrive and your morning coffee feel like a small ritual. The kitchen is genuinely good: shaker cabinets, stainless appliances, built-in microwave, dishwasher, garbage disposal, marble-style countertops, white subway-tile backsplash, recessed lighting throughout. Hardwood floors run the length of the apartment.

The bedroom is set behind sliding barn-style doors and faces the back, so weekend energy on 18th Street stays outside. \textbf{You'll see the bedroom in the photos as currently empty} because I sleep on a Japanese futon that I roll out at night and put away in the morning. Before I leave I'll bring in a proper bed (queen, frame and mattress, ready for you).

The bathroom is the surprise of the place. Double vanity with two sinks and two mirrors on a marble counter, lots of drawer storage, and a generous tiled walk-in shower with a built-in niche shelf for shampoos. A stacked washer-dryer sits just outside the bathroom so you never see a laundromat.

At 599 sq ft (about 56 m\textsuperscript{2}) the open layout makes it feel much larger than the number suggests. \textbf{The apartment is currently arranged the way I like it: open, airy, lots of empty floor, not a stick of clutter, set up for one person who wanted to breathe.} The dining piece is a foldable table that takes about 20 seconds to put away. I'm flexible on the furniture, we can figure out together what works for your life.

The couch is a modular piece that pulls apart, rearranges, or unfolds into a flat surface big enough to nap on or seat half a dozen friends for a movie night. It's deeply comfortable, easy to clean, and the kind of furniture that quietly does a lot of the work in such a space.

Central A/C keeps it comfortable through DC summers, forced-air heat through the winters. Gas, water, trash, electricity, internet, and a robot vacuum that handles the floors all stay with the apartment.

For movie nights, there's a projector that throws a huge image onto the long white wall in the living room. Better than a TV and the cinema rolled together when friends come over.

You won't need a car. The 42 bus runs straight downtown from the corner, three Capital Bikeshare stations sit within a block, and the walk score is 99. If you'd like, I'll leave the bike too.

\textbf{The apartment is registered with the DC pet community.} If you ever want to foster a cat through City Dogs \& City Kitties DC, I'm happy to put you in touch. The unit has already been vetted as a foster-friendly home.

\section{The neighborhood}\label{sec:nbh}

\eyebrow{Why I love living here, and why you will too}

Adams Morgan is the closest thing DC has to a real \textbf{15-minute city}. Almost everything you need (groceries, the gym, the dog park, the bookshop, your coffee place, dinner, drinks, the dentist) sits inside a quarter-mile radius (roughly 400 m for my international friends) of the front door. Most of DC is built around commuting; here you'll see the same faces in line for coffee, at the dog park, on the basketball court at Marie Reed, browsing the shelves at Lost City Books, at the Saturday farmers market downstairs. It's small (0.47 sq mi $\cdot$ 1.2 km\textsuperscript{2}, about 17.000 people), it's dense, and almost everyone walks. Walk Score 99, Bike Score 92, Transit Score 80.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  enhanced, colback={brandred!8}, colframe=brandred, boxrule=0pt,
  borderline west={3pt}{0pt}{brandred},
  arc=2pt, left=14pt, right=14pt, top=10pt, bottom=10pt,
]
\textbf{Honest disclaimer.} If what you want is a quiet, suburban kind of calm, this isn't the spot. Adams Morgan has a pulse: bars, music, foot traffic, weekend crowds. But here's the thing about this specific apartment: the bedroom faces the back, the windows don't face 18th Street, and the building has just four units. Inside, you'll be surprised how peaceful it is. So if you want a vibrant neighborhood you can step into when you feel like it, and a quiet place to come home to when you don't, this is your spot.
\end{tcolorbox}

The center of it is the corner of 18th Street and Columbia Road. From the front door you're a block from Ethiopian dinners at Tsehay and Elfegne, kebabs at Lapis a few minutes down Columbia, pasta at Reveler's Hour, and a Saturday farmers market that sets up basically downstairs. The American Planning Association named the neighborhood one of America's ``great neighborhoods'' in 2014. Adams Morgan Day, the September street festival, has run since 1978 and draws tens of thousands every year.

For someone who likes to move around, the geography is unbeatable. Rock Creek Park (1.700 acres / 690 hectares of trails) is a five-minute walk west. Marie Reed Recreation Center has an indoor pool, a soccer turf, and basketball courts inside the neighborhood. Walter Pierce Park has the dog crowd in the morning and a full-size soccer field in the evening. There are gyms, yoga studios, Solidcore, run clubs, pickup leagues, and a Capital Bikeshare station basically at the door.

The thing the demographic profile doesn't capture is the texture. It's a place where the barista at Tryst pulls your double espresso before you've fully said hello, where you can take a torn jacket to Do\~na Rosa down the block and have it back the next day, and where three months in you'll already know half the names on the block.

\subsection*{A short history}

Colonial-era estates after DC was created in 1791. Electric streetcars arrived in 1903 and the area grew into upper- and middle-class neighborhoods. In 1948, Charles Lazarus founded Toys ``R'' Us here. The name comes from two formerly segregated elementary schools, all-Black Thomas P. Morgan and all-white John Quincy Adams, which merged in 1955 following racial desegregation. From 2010 to 2012 the city reconstructed 18th Street NW with wider sidewalks and bike infrastructure. The American Planning Association named Adams Morgan one of the country's ``great neighborhoods'' in 2014. It has steadily attracted new investment and a wave of younger residents while keeping much of its original character intact, and is now also one of DC's recognized hubs of LGBTQ culture.

\subsection*{The longer version}

\subsubsection*{Daily life is a half-mile radius}
Almost everything you need is within a half-mile of the door. The ``Social Safeway'' at 1747 Columbia Road is the workhorse, high volume, often crowded. Harris Teeter on Kalorama is the calmer shop a few blocks away. \textbf{Yes! Organic Market} has been here since 1970. \textbf{Adams Morgan Ace Hardware} is housed in the converted Ontario Theatre, a beautiful old movie house that's now part hardware store, part neighborhood plant shop. \textbf{So's Your Mom} is the deli locals tell newcomers about, get the Italian sub.

\subsubsection*{What you'll be eating}
\textbf{Lapis}, an Afghan bistro a few minutes' walk down Columbia Road, is my weeknight default. Kebabs, rice, the kind of plates you order without thinking. Beyond that, the neighborhood's signature is Ethiopian: \textbf{Tsehay} and \textbf{Elfegne} are a block apart, eat with your hands, leave full, never spend a fortune. \textbf{Federalist Pig} does the best smoked brisket in DC and sells out by 8pm. \textbf{Amsterdam Falafelshop} stays open past bar close. For something fancier, \textbf{Tail Up Goat} and its sister \textbf{Reveler's Hour} are both walking distance.

\subsubsection*{Nightlife you'll actually use}
\textbf{Jack Rose} holds the largest whiskey collection in the country (2.700+ bottles). \textbf{Roofers Union}'s rooftop is peak summer. \textbf{Tiki on 18th} makes well-made tropical drinks. \textbf{Madam's Organ} has live blues seven nights a week (running since 1992) and is two doors down. \textbf{Bossa} is one of the very few DC bars where people actually dance. \textbf{Code Red} is the hidden cocktail bar. A liquor-license moratorium from 2000 capped the bar count at around 100, which keeps the strip dense without sprawling.

\subsubsection*{Outdoors and sport, more than you'd guess}
\textbf{Rock Creek Park} opens up 1.700 acres (690 hectares) of trails a five-minute walk west. \textbf{Marie Reed Recreation Center} has an indoor pool, soccer turf, and basketball courts. \textbf{Walter Pierce Park} has a full-size soccer field. \textbf{Solidcore}, several yoga studios, gyms, and run clubs are all within ten minutes. \textbf{Meridian Hill}'s Sunday drum circle (3pm to 5pm) is the neighborhood at its most communal.

\subsubsection*{The metro gap, and why it doesn't matter}
Adams Morgan doesn't have its own metro stop. The neighborhood sits between Woodley Park (Red Line) and Columbia Heights (Green/Yellow), both about 12 minutes away. The real answer is the bus: the \textbf{42, 43, 90, 92, 96, H1, L2, S2, and S9} all run frequently down Columbia Road, 16th Street, and Florida Avenue. Capital Bikeshare is exceptionally dense, with multiple stations within a block. The apartment comes with a bike if you'd like it.

\subsubsection*{Daytime, when the sun's out}
The \textbf{Mama Ayesha's mural} on Calvert Street, a row of every US president painted alongside the restaurant's matriarch, is one of the most photographed walls in DC. \textbf{Sunday drum circle at Meridian Hill}: bring a blanket, stay until sunset. \textbf{Adams Morgan Day} every September. \textbf{Adams Morgan PorchFest} happens twice a year (spring and fall): bands play on residential porches, you walk between sets, drink in hand. The most fun anyone has all year.

\subsubsection*{The shops that aren't chains}
The local hero is \textbf{Lost City Books} (formerly Idle Time): independent, deeply curated, regular author talks. \textbf{Mercedes Bien Vintage} is open weekends only since the 80s. \textbf{Smash Records} is a punk vinyl institution since 1984. The neighborhood's murals (including ``Carry the Rainbow on Your Shoulders'' and ``The Servant Christ'') are among the most documented in the city. \textbf{DC Arts Center} hosts indie performance and exhibition work.
