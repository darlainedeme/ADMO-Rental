What it's like

The space, in plain words.

It's unit 4 in a three-story brick condo from 1900. Just four apartments in the whole building, and neighbors who actually say hi in the stairwell. That bit matters more than people realize: this isn't a 200-unit tower where you ride the elevator next to strangers. There's nobody upstairs (you're on the top floor, so no one stomps on your head), and you'll know who lives in front and downstairs by the second week.

Inside, the kitchen and living area share one open, light-flooded room with two big windows. The windows do not face 18th Street, which is the point: you get the daylight without the sidewalk noise. The apartment gets a remarkable amount of natural light, especially in the afternoon, the kind that makes plants thrive and your morning coffee feel like a small ritual. The kitchen is genuinely good: shaker cabinets, stainless appliances, built-in microwave, dishwasher, garbage disposal, marble-style countertops, white subway-tile backsplash, recessed lighting throughout. Hardwood floors run the length of the apartment.

The bedroom is set behind sliding barn-style doors and faces the back, so weekend energy on 18th Street stays outside. You'll see the bedroom in the photos as currently empty because I sleep on a Japanese futon that I roll out at night and put away in the morning. Before I leave I'll bring in a proper bed (queen, frame and mattress, ready for you). The earlier bedroom photos with the bed and side tables show how it looked before I moved in, so you can picture both arrangements.

The bathroom is the surprise of the place. Double vanity with two sinks and two mirrors on a marble counter, lots of drawer storage, and a generous tiled walk-in shower with a built-in niche shelf for shampoos. A stacked washer-dryer sits just outside the bathroom so you never see a laundromat.

At 599 sq ft (about 56 m²) the open layout makes it feel much larger than the number suggests. The apartment is currently arranged the way I like it: open, airy, lots of empty floor, not a stick of clutter, set up for one person who wanted to breathe. The dining piece is a foldable table that takes about 20 seconds to put away. I'm flexible on the furniture, we can figure out together what works for your life and I'll add or remove pieces accordingly.

The couch is a modular piece that pulls apart, rearranges, or unfolds into a flat surface big enough to nap on or seat half a dozen friends for a movie night. It's deeply comfortable, easy to clean, and the kind of furniture that quietly does a lot of the work in such a space.

Central A/C keeps it comfortable through DC summers, forced-air heat through the winters. Gas, water, trash, and a robot vacuum that handles the floors all stay with the apartment.

For movie nights, there's a projector that throws a huge image onto the long white wall in the living room. Better than a TV and the cinema rolled together when friends come over.

You won't need a car. The 42 bus runs straight downtown from the corner, three Capital Bikeshare stations sit within a block, and the walk score is 99. If you'd like, I'll leave the bike too. It's the fastest way around the neighborhood and gets you to Rock Creek trails in about five minutes.

The apartment is registered with the DC pet community. If you ever want to foster a cat through City Dogs & City Kitties DC, I'm happy to put you in touch. The unit has already been vetted as a foster-friendly home.
